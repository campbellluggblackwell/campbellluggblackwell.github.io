Campbells, Luggs & Blackwells of Nelson, PA

ca 1952 Clipping from The Elkland Journal1.

(News clip courtesy of Beulah's granddaughters Dortha RICE Davenport and Eileen RICE Harris)

AN OLD SKILL FROM AN OLD FAMILY

The soft thump-bump of an ancient loom can be heard any warm afternoon within a stone's throw of the Elkland business section where beautifully designed rugs flow through the hands of Mrs. Beulah Rice who has been using this family loom for more than 50 years.

Mrs. Beulah Rice is pictured here with a section of the rug she is currently making after a Swedish pattern given her by Marian Nelson, former Elkland resident now living in Utah. Without question, the oldest loom still in use in this area and perhaps the first loom ever used in Tioga County [PA], the old weaving machine is now erected in a garage at the rear of Hennings Hardware. Here, weather permitting, you will find Mrs. Rice turning out custom built rugs---a skill she has practiced since she was eleven years of age.

Mrs. Rice, who lives in the Warburton apartments, moved here from Farmington [Twp.] 7 years ago. She has been employed intermittently by the Kayser Company2 but has done custom weaving and baking as a major source of income. Her rugs are marketed locally at from $5 to $63 and require about a day to weave.

She was born on the Cook homestead north of Nelson at the New York State line,4 the daughter of Burton and Jennie (Mary Jane) Swan Cook. The loom was an integral part of the Cook household and was used by both her grandmother and mother. While still a little girl, she learned to operate the loom and make pattern changes, which requires more than a day of skillful effort.

Many thousands of wool and cotton rugs have passed through her nimble fingers over the years----rag carpeting, which was standard wall to wall covering 50 years ago, hit and miss rugs, plaid and stripes. The Parkhurst Memorial Presbyterian Church of Elkland for years raised funds through the sale of her rugs.

She continued with her weaving while raising four sons, Donald, James, Burton and Harry Rice, all well-known local men.

Mrs. Rice does not know the exact age of the loom, which has been in her family for at least four generations. She is a direct descendent [sic] of pioneer David Cook, who had moved into the Corning-Lindley frontier in 1780 and is the great-great granddaughter of Levi Cook, his son, the Cowanesque Valley's first blacksmith who is known to have done work for James Strawbridge at Academy Corners in 1791.


Whether the loom came here with David Cook, was built by his son Levi Cook or his grandson James Cook, is not known, however, Mrs. Rice does know that her mother and grandmother learned their skills on the device.

The loom was well worn and patched when Mrs. Rice first saw it as a child. She well remembers that her family obtained wool for the loom from a carding mill located at Academy Corners.

Mrs. Rice is related directly and indirectly to many of the early families who settled this are the Cooks, the Swans, Campbell's and Travers. Her grandfather Samuel Swan operated the Addison-Elkland stage; another relative erected the Hotel Signor in December of 1875, The forges and carpenter shops of the Cooks are long gone but the old loom which thumps and bumps Elkland's November air remains to remember the long line of skilled craftsmen and Workmen who came from the blood streams of the men who hued the ancient loom from native ash.
